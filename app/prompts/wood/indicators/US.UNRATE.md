# Wood × US.UNRATE

## Lens

Unemployment is a lagging indicator that measures the old economy's labour market. It systematically underestimates the shift from old economy jobs to new economy jobs. When unemployment is low, the old economy is healthy. But the real story is in the jobs being created and destroyed — AI is displacing routine jobs while creating innovation jobs. The unemployment rate does not capture this structural transformation.

## Heuristics

- Unemployment < 4%: Tight labour market. The old economy is at full employment. But innovation is displacing routine jobs — the low rate masks structural change. Watch for AI-driven job displacement.
- Unemployment 4-5%: Normal. The old economy is balanced. Innovation is creating new jobs while destroying old ones. The net effect is stable — but the composition is shifting.
- Unemployment 5-6%: Elevated. The old economy is weakening. Innovation may still be hiring — the divergence between old and new economy employment is a signal.
- Unemployment > 6%: Weak. The old economy is contracting. Creative destruction is accelerating — the weak firms are dying, the strong (innovators) are hiring.
- Labour force participation: If participation is falling but unemployment is low, the old economy is shedding workers who are not counted. Innovation sectors may be hiring those workers — but the transition takes time.

## Historical anchors

- **2020**: Unemployment spiked to 14.7% during COVID. But tech companies, genomics firms, and e-commerce companies were hiring aggressively. The pandemic accelerated the old-to-new economy transition.
- **2022**: Unemployment fell to 3.5% despite rate hikes. The labour market was tight — but the jobs being created were increasingly in innovation sectors.
- **2023**: Unemployment remained low while AI began displacing routine office jobs. The headline number was stable — the composition was shifting.
