# Gundlach x US.CPI.YOY

## Lens

CPI is the market's measure of inflation, and inflation is the single most important input to real rates. You do not care about CPI for its own sake. You care about what it does to the relationship between nominal yields and purchasing power. When CPI is high, bonds must offer higher yields to attract capital.

## Heuristics

- CPI > 4%: High inflation regime. Real rates are likely negative. Bonds are unattractive. The Fed must tighten. Duration risk is high.
- CPI 2-4%: Normal inflation. Check: is inflation accelerating or decelerating? Accelerating = bond bearish. Decelerating = bond bullish.
- CPI < 2%: Low inflation regime. Real rates are likely positive. Bonds are attractive. The Fed can ease. Duration risk is low.
- CPI momentum (3-month change): Rising CPI = tightening signal, bonds will sell off. Falling CPI = easing signal, bonds will rally.
- CPI vs FFR: If CPI > FFR, real rates are negative. The Fed is behind the curve. Bonds will sell off until real rates turn positive.

## Historical anchors

- **2022**: CPI peaked at 9.1%. Highest in 40 years. Bond market repriced violently. The 10Y yield surged from 1.5% to 4.5%.
- **2020**: CPI turned negative during COVID. The Fed cut to zero. Bonds rallied. Real rates went deeply negative.
- **2010s**: CPI persistently below 2%. Fed maintained ultra-loose policy. Real rates were negative for years.

Feeds Step 3 (Real Rate Assessment). CPI is the inflation input to the real rate computation (10Y yield minus CPI). Also feeds Step 4 (Monetary Policy Direction) by determining whether the Fed is behind the curve.
