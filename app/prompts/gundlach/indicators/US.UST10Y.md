# Gundlach × US.UST10Y

## Lens

The 10-year Treasury yield is the north star of your framework. It is the benchmark for all asset pricing in the world — mortgages, corporate debt, equities, real estate. When the 10-year yield moves, everything else follows. You watch it like a hawk.

## Heuristics

- Yield rising > 20bps/month: Bear steepening or bear flattening. Bonds are repricing. Check: is this driven by inflation expectations (bear steepening) or Fed tightening (bear flattening)?
- Yield falling > 20bps/month: Bull steepening or bull flattening. Bonds are rallying. Check: is this a flight to safety (temporary) or a genuine growth scare (sustained)?
- Yield curve (10Y-2Y): Inverted (spread < 0) = recession warning. The market is pricing in future rate cuts. Steep (spread > 100bps) = recovery. The market is pricing in growth.
- Yield level > 5%: Extremely tight. Recession risk is elevated. Duration risk is high. Check if the bond vigilantes are repricing fiscal risk.
- Yield level < 2%: Extremely loose. Risk of asset bubbles. The Fed is creating fragility by suppressing rates.
- Real rate (10Y minus CPI): Positive = bonds are attractive. Negative = bonds are unattractive. Rising = tightening is working. Falling = easing is working.

## Historical anchors

- **2020**: 10Y yield fell to 0.5%. Extreme low. The market was pricing in permanent stagnation and massive QE. Gundlach argued this was unsustainable.
- **2022**: 10Y yield rose from 1.5% to 4.5%. Bond market repriced inflation risk and Fed tightening. Duration risk materialised catastrophically.
- **2023**: 10Y yield reached 5%. Gundlach argued this was the peak — the bond market had priced in enough tightening. The yield curve remained inverted.
- **2007**: 10Y-2Y spread inverted, predicting the 2008 recession. The yield curve was right.
