# Gundlach × US.FFR

## Lens

The Fed funds rate is the anchor of the short end of the curve. The Fed sets it. The market sets the long end. When the Fed and the market disagree, the market wins — always. You watch the gap between FFR and the 10-year yield to determine whether the Fed is ahead of or behind the curve.

## Heuristics

- FFR rising: Tightening cycle. Shorten duration. Credit spreads will eventually widen. The curve will flatten or invert.
- FFR falling: Easing cycle. Extend duration. Credit spreads will eventually tighten. The curve will steepen.
- FFR stable: No change signal. Rely on curve shape and credit spreads.
- FFR > 5%: Tight policy. Recession risk increases. Check if the yield curve is inverted — if so, the Fed has overtightened.
- FFR < 1%: Easy policy. Risk of asset bubbles. The Fed is creating fragility. Duration is being suppressed.
- FFR vs 10Y spread: If FFR > 10Y (inverted policy curve), the Fed is behind the curve or the market is pricing in a recession. Either way, the bond market is telling the truth.

## Historical anchors

- **2008-2015**: FFR at zero for 7 years. Created massive reach for yield and moral hazard. The long end refused to follow — the market was signaling that zero rates were unsustainable.
- **2022-2023**: FFR rose from 0% to 5.5% in 18 months. The fastest tightening cycle in decades. The yield curve inverted deeply — the market was pricing in the recession the Fed was creating.
- **2020**: FFR cut to zero overnight. The long end barely moved — the market had already priced in the crisis.

## Playbook step

Feeds Step 4 (Monetary Policy Direction). FFR level and direction determine the policy regime and how the short end of the curve is positioned. The gap between FFR and 10Y yield is the key signal for whether the Fed is ahead of or behind the curve.
