# Dalio — Analytical Framework

## The Big Cycle of Nations
Every nation follows a repeating cycle of rise and decline, driven by eight key measures:
1. **Education** — educated populations produce more productive economies
2. **Competitiveness** — low-cost, high-quality production attracts global capital
3. **Innovation and technology** — new technologies create new industries and wealth
4. **Economic output** — GDP share of global output reflects power
5. **Share of world trade** — trade dominance signals economic centrality
6. **Military strength** — military power protects trade routes and economic interests
7. **Financial center status** — the dominant financial center attracts global capital
8. **Reserve currency status** — the reserve currency allows unlimited borrowing in your own currency

When all eight are rising, the nation is ascendant. When they peak and start declining, the nation enters a period of internal disorder and eventual decline. The transition from one dominant power to another is the most dangerous period in global economics.

## Short-Term Debt Cycle Phases
1. **Early expansion** — credit is easy, earnings grow, stock prices rise
2. **Mid expansion** — growth is strong, inflation emerges, central bank tightens
3. **Late expansion** — credit is tight, growth slows, recession approaches
4. **Recession** — credit contracts, earnings fall, stock prices decline
5. **Recovery** — central bank eases, credit expands, cycle restarts

## All-Weather Portfolio Principle
Build a portfolio that performs reasonably well in all four economic environments:
- **Rising growth + Rising inflation** → Equities, TIPS, commodities
- **Rising growth + Falling inflation** → Equities, nominal bonds
- **Falling growth + Rising inflation** → TIPS, commodities, gold
- **Falling growth + Falling inflation** → Nominal bonds, gold

## Risk Parity
Do not allocate 60% equities / 40% bonds because that is the traditional split. Instead, allocate based on risk contribution. Equities have roughly 3x the volatility of bonds. A 60/40 portfolio is actually 90% equity risk. A true risk-parity portfolio might be 30% equities, 50% bonds, 20% commodities — each contributing equally to total portfolio risk.
