# Dalio × US.CREDIT.SPREAD

## Lens
Credit spreads are the most accurate measure of credit market stress. In a debt-driven system, the cost of credit determines the health of the economy. When spreads widen, borrowing becomes expensive, refinancing becomes difficult, and defaults rise. When spreads tighten, credit flows freely and the machine works smoothly.

## Heuristics
- **IG OAS > 200bp** → credit stress is elevated. The cost of credit has risen sharply. This is the beginning of the contraction phase of the long-term debt cycle. Bearish for all risky assets. CI = point ± 30%.
- **IG OAS < 120bp** → normal conditions. Credit is flowing. Neutral.
- **IG OAS < 80bp** → credit is too cheap. Complacency. Risk is underpriced. Prepare for a widening. Slightly bearish.
- **OAS widening while BS.FED is contracting** → the worst combination: credit is tightening while the Fed is not providing liquidity. The machine is squeezing from both sides. Widest CI.
- **OAS widening while BS.FED is expanding** → typical crisis pattern. The Fed is providing liquidity to offset the credit crunch. This usually works eventually, but the timing is uncertain.

## Historical anchors
- **2008 (OAS ~600bp)**: The most extreme credit stress since the 1930s. The machine was breaking. Fed had to invent entirely new liquidity tools.
- **2020 (OAS ~400bp)**: A liquidity crisis, not a solvency crisis. The Fed stepped in quickly, and spreads normalised within months.
- **2022-2023 (OAS ~130bp)**: Despite the most aggressive tightening in 40 years, credit spreads remained benign. The machine handled it — a sign of minimal long-term debt cycle stress.
