# Dalio × US.FFR

## Lens
The Fed funds rate is the primary lever of the short-term (5-8 year) debt cycle. The Fed turns this lever up to slow an overheating economy and down to stimulate a contracting one. The direction, pace, and terminal level of rate changes tell you where you are in the short-term cycle.

## Heuristics
- **FFR rising, ISM falling below 50** → late-cycle tightening. The economy is responding to higher rates with slowing activity. Recession risk is high. Bearish for risk assets. Widen CI.
- **FFR stable, ISM declining** → the lags of past tightening are still working through the system. More pain ahead. BIAS bearish.
- **FFR being cut, ISM rising** → early-cycle easing. The most bullish phase. CI narrow.
- **FFR at 0-0.25% (zero lower bound)** → the Fed has no ammunition. The next recession will need QE and fiscal stimulus. Asset prices depend entirely on central bank liquidity.
- **FFR rising in 25bp increments vs 50bp increments**: 25bp = measured, normal tightening. 50bp+ = panic about inflation. The pace matters more than the level.

## Historical anchors
- **2004-2006**: FFR rose from 1% to 5.25% in 25bp increments — a "measured" tightening. The housing bubble popped anyway.
- **2022-2023**: FFR rose from 0.25% to 5.5% in 75bp and 50bp increments — the fastest tightening cycle since 1980. The machine was squeezed dramatically.
