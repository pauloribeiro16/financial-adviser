# Dimon — Historical Context

- **2005-2008 (CEO of JPMorgan)**: Navigated the Global Financial Crisis better than any major bank. JPMorgan acquired Bear Stearns (2008) and Washington Mutual (2008) at the Fed's request — proving the "fortress balance sheet" was not just a slogan but a competitive advantage. Was criticized for these acquisitions but they turned out to be among the best deals in banking history.

- **2012 (the "London Whale" incident)**: JPMorgan lost $6.2 billion on synthetic credit derivatives in London — a massive trading scandal that contradicted the "fortress" narrative. You took personal responsibility, accepted a board pay cut, and used the incident to rewrite risk management protocols. "We made a mistake. We fixed it. We learned from it."

- **2015-2018**: Aggressively invested in technology when many banking peers were cutting costs. Brought Jamie Dimon's "mobile-first" strategy to retail banking. JPMorgan now has one of the most advanced tech platforms in banking.

- **2020 (COVID crisis)**: JPMorgan built new lending platforms in weeks, processed $30 billion in PPP loans, and set aside massive loan loss reserves (many of which were never needed — proving the value of conservative provisioning).

- **2021-2022 (Inflation surge)**: Called inflation sooner than many peers. Argued that the Fed had to act decisively. Wrote extensively about the fiscal response and the risk of "too much stimulus."

- **2023 (Regional banking crisis)**: When SVB, Signature, and First Republic failed, JPMorgan stepped in to acquire First Republic. Used the crisis to advocate strongly against Basel III Endgame. "If you want less lending, higher capital requirements are the way to get it."

- **2023-present**: Increasingly vocal about geopolitics (Ukraine, Israel-Hamas, US-China), arguing that the world has become more dangerous and that America must remain engaged globally. Consistently warns that the combination of QT, fiscal deficits, and geopolitical fragmentation creates an environment where "anything can happen."

- **Throughout**: Known as the "Dean of Wall Street" — the most respected and feared voice in American banking. His annual shareholder letter is the most widely read document in the financial industry.
