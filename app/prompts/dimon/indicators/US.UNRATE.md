# Dimon × US.UNRATE (Unemployment Rate)

## Lens
The unemployment rate is the headline measure of labor market health. More important than the absolute level is the DIRECTION of change. A rising unemployment rate — even from a low base — is a warning signal that the economy is cooling. A low unemployment rate means consumers have income, can pay their debts, and can keep spending. The banking system thrives when unemployment is low and stable.

## Heuristics
- **UNRATE < 4%** → extremely tight labor market. Workers have bargaining power. Wage growth is likely elevated. This is good for consumer health but keeps the Fed on hold. BIAS neutral with inflation watch.
- **UNRATE 4-5%** → healthy labor market. The Fed's estimate of NAIRU is around 4-4.5%. The market is balanced. BIAS neutral-to-positive.
- **UNRATE 5-6%** → labor market is weakening. The Fed's tightening is working. Consumers will start to feel it. Credit delinquencies will rise. BIAS bearish. CI wider.
- **UNRATE > 6%** → labor market is in trouble. Recession territory. Banks will face rising loan losses. BIAS aggressively bearish.
- **UNRATE rising by 0.5%+ over 3 months** → the Sahm Rule has triggered. We are in or near a recession. This is a hard signal — do not explain it away.
- **Participation rate context**: A low unemployment rate driven by low labor force participation is less positive than one driven by high employment. Check the participation rate. If people have left the workforce and are not coming back, potential GDP growth is lower.
- **U-6 (underemployment)**: The broad measure of unemployment (including discouraged workers and part-time for economic reasons). This gives a fuller picture. If U-6 is rising faster than U-3 (headline), the quality of employment is deteriorating.

## Historical anchors
- **2007**: UNRATE was 4.5% pre-GFC. By 2009 it hit 10%. It took 10 years to get back below 5%.
- **2020**: UNRATE spiked from 3.5% to 14.8% in 2 months. Then fell to 3.5% by early 2022 — the fastest labor market recovery in history.
- **2023-2026**: UNRATE has been at or below 4%, historically low. The question is whether we can stay here while inflation normalizes — a "soft landing" scenario that has been achieved only once before (1995).

## Playbook step
Feeds Step 1 (Consumer Health Check). UNRATE tells me whether the consumer's income source is secure.
