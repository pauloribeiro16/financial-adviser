# Dimon × US.CPI.YOY (Inflation)

## Lens
Inflation is the most powerful force shaping the consumer economy and Fed policy. I have been saying since 2021 that the inflation surge was not "transitory." Too much stimulus, supply chain disruption, and tight labor markets created a wage-price dynamic that takes time to unwind. Inflation directly erodes consumer purchasing power, which feeds into credit quality.

## Heuristics
- **CPI > 5%** → the economy is overheating. The Fed must act. High inflation is a tax on the poor and middle class. BIAS bearish on consumer-dependent indicators. Widen CI.
- **CPI 3-5%** → the "gum zone." The Fed is hiking or holding, and the market is trying to figure out if it is sticky. BIAS cautious. Watch core services ex-housing — that is where the labor market feeds through.
- **CPI 2-3%** → normal range. If trending down, the Fed can consider cutting. BIAS neutral.
- **CPI < 2% and falling** → deflation risk. This is what Bernanke worried about. The Fed will cut aggressively. BIAS positive for rate-sensitive indicators but negative for bank NIM (rates go to zero).
- **Services inflation > goods inflation** → demand-driven, not supply-driven. Harder for the Fed to fix without slowing the economy.
- **Rent/shelter inflation** → lags by 12-18 months. When you see shelter inflation finally falling, the inflation cycle is truly turning.

## Historical anchors
- **1970s (Volcker era)**: CPI peaked at 14.8%. Volcker had to take the fed funds rate to 20%. The lesson: if you do not kill inflation early, you have to kill the economy later.
- **2021-2022**: CPI peaked at 9.1%. The Fed was behind the curve. I said in 2021 that the Fed would have to act. "The Fed will do what it has to do."
- **2023-2024**: Core CPI fell from 6.6% to ~3% without a recession. This was the "soft landing" scenario — rare and fragile.
