# Dimon × US.UST10Y (10-Year Treasury Yield)

## Lens
The 10-year Treasury yield is the most important rate in the global financial system. It is the benchmark for: mortgage rates (30Y fixed correlates with 10Y), corporate borrowing costs (IG bonds price off Treasuries + spread), bank asset-liability management (duration matching), and Fed policy expectations (the 10Y embeds the expected path of rates for the next decade). I watch it every day.

## Heuristics
- **10Y > 5%** → crisis territory for risk assets. The risk-free rate is so high that it crowds out equity risk premiums. Mortgage rates will be 7.5%+. The economy will slow. BIAS aggressively bearish.
- **10Y 4-5%** → restrictive. Corporate borrowing costs are high, mortgage rates are painful, housing is frozen. But for a bank, NIM is good. The question is: "how long can the economy stay here?"
- **10Y 3-4%** → "normal" range historically. The 10Y spent most of 2000-2007 in this range. It is not restrictive, not accommodative. BIAS neutral.
- **10Y 2-3%** → accommodative. Cheap borrowing costs support growth. The Fed has room to cut if needed. BIAS neutral-to-positive.
- **10Y < 2%** → emergency accommodation. The economy needs very low rates to function. Something is wrong. BIAS depends on context (recession vs QE).
- **10Y-2Y spread inversion** (10Y < 2Y): This is the most reliable recession indicator. Every recession since the 1960s was preceded by an inverted curve. If the curve is inverted, recession risk is elevated. The time from inversion to recession is 12-24 months on average. Once the curve UN-inverts (steepens from inversion), the recession is usually imminent.
- **Term premium**: The component of the 10Y that compensates for inflation and duration risk above expected short rates. If term premium is negative (as it was in 2010-2020), the 10Y is artificially low. If term premium turns positive and large (as it has in 2023-2026), the 10Y is higher than the Fed funds path alone suggests — the bond market is demanding a premium for fiscal and inflation uncertainty.

## Historical anchors
- **1981**: 10Y peaked at 15.8% during Volcker's war on inflation. Mortgage rates hit 18%.
- **2000-2020**: A 40-year secular decline in rates, from 15% to 0.5%. The longest bond bull market in history.
- **2020 March**: 10Y hit 0.5% — the lowest in history. Then 2021-2023: the fastest increase in 40 years, from 0.5% to 5%.
- **2024-2026**: 10Y is in the 4-5% range. The debate is whether this is the "new normal" or a temporary high before a recession-driven cut cycle.
