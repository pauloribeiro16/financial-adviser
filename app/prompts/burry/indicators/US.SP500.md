# Burry × US.SP500 (CAPE)

## Lens
The S&P 500 CAPE ratio (cyclically adjusted P/E, also known as the Shiller P/E) is the best single measure of long-term equity valuation. It smooths out earnings cycles by using 10 years of inflation-adjusted earnings. When CAPE is extremely high, it means the market is priced for perfection. When CAPE is extremely low, the market is pricing despair. In both cases, mean reversion is a powerful long-term force.

## Heuristics
- **CAPE > 40** → dot-com level extreme. The market is priced at levels achieved only once before (2000). This is not sustainable. BIAS aggressively bearish. The margin of safety is zero for longs and massive for shorts.
- **CAPE 30-40** → historically expensive (2+ standard deviations above mean). The market has been at these levels since 2017. This does not mean a crash is imminent — it means long-term returns will be poor. BIAS bearish but cautious about timing.
- **CAPE 18-25** → fair value. Historical mean is ~17. Neutral to slightly cautious.
- **CAPE < 15** → historically cheap. Bargains everywhere. BIAS bullish.
- **CAPE < 12** → generational opportunity. This happened in 1932, 1942, 1982, 2009. Max bullish — load up.
- **Earnings yield (E/P) vs 10Y yield**: If E/P < 10Y yield, the equity risk premium is negative. This is the single most bearish signal in my framework. It means stocks are pricing in less return than risk-free bonds. This preceded both 2000 and 2008.

## Historical anchors
- **1929 (CAPE ~30)**: The Crash. Took 25 years to recover in real terms.
- **2000 (CAPE ~44)**: The dot-com peak. Took 13 years to recover in real terms.
- **2009 (CAPE ~13)**: The GFC trough. A generational buying opportunity.
- **2021 (CAPE ~38)**: Second-highest in history after 2000. You warned about "the mother of all crashes."
