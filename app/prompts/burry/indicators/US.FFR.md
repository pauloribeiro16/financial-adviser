# Burry × US.FFR

## Lens
The Fed funds rate is the cost of leverage. Low rates encourage borrowing, which inflates asset prices and creates bubbles. High rates suppress borrowing, which deflates asset prices and pops bubbles. The level matters, but the trajectory matters more.

## Heuristics
- **FFR < 1% for extended period** → the cost of leverage is near zero. This encourages maximum risk-taking. Bubbles form when rates are low and stay low. BEARISH — the easy money creates the next crisis.
- **FFR > 5%** → tight money. The cost of borrowing is high. Economic activity slows. If rates have risen rapidly (>300bp in 12 months), something will break.
- **FFR rising from low levels (e.g. 0.25% → 2%)** → the tightening phase. This is when the most overleveraged parts of the market crack first. Watch for hedge fund failures, bank runs, or derivatives dislocations.
- **FFR being cut from high levels** → the Fed is reacting to a problem. This is never good. Rate cuts in isolation are not a buy signal — the reason for the cuts matters.
- **FFR near zero while inflation is above target** → negative real rates. The Fed is using financial repression to stimulate the economy. This is distorting asset prices. BEARISH long-term.

## Historical anchors
- **2003-2004 (FFR 1%)**: The low rates that fuelled the housing bubble. Greenspan kept rates low too long.
- **2006 (FFR 5.25%)**: The peak that popped the housing bubble. The lag effect: the housing market peaked in 2006 but didn't crash until 2007-2008.
- **2022-2023 (FFR 0 → 5.5%)**: The fastest increase since 1980. Caused the Silicon Valley Bank failure in 2023.
