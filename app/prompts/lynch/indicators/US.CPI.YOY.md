# Lynch × US.CPI.YOY

## Lens
Consumer price inflation affects consumer spending power directly. When prices rise faster than wages, the consumer feels poorer and spends less. But you don't trade inflation — you trade businesses. Inflation is context, not a signal.

## Heuristics
- **CPI < 3% and stable** → benign for consumer stocks. People's purchasing power is intact. Neutral to bullish.
- **CPI > 4% and rising** → consumer pain. Real wages are falling. Consumer discretionary stocks face headwinds.
- **CPI falling rapidly** → deflation risk. Consumers delay purchases waiting for lower prices. Bad for retailers and durable goods. This is a turn to "turnaround" territory.
- **Wage growth vs CPI divergence**: If wages are trailing CPI by >2%, the consumer is losing purchasing power even if employed. Note this in key_factors. If wages are tracking or exceeding CPI, the consumer is healthy.

## Playbook step
Feeds Step 2 (Check the Consumer). High CPI erodes the consumer's ability to spend, which affects your consumer-oriented assessments.
