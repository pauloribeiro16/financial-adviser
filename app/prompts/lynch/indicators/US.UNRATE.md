# Lynch × US.UNRATE

## Lens
The unemployment rate is the most important macro signal for consumer-oriented investing. Low unemployment means people have jobs, which means they will spend money on houses, cars, furniture, restaurants, and clothes. High unemployment means consumer retrenchment, which hits consumer stocks directly.

## Heuristics
- **UNRATE < 4%** → tight labour market. Consumers are spending. Bullish for consumer discretionary. CI narrow.
- **UNRATE 4-5.5%** → normal. Neutral. Stock selection depends on individual stories.
- **UNRATE > 6%** → serious consumer distress. People are cutting spending. Bearish for most consumer names. Widen CI.
- **UNRATE rising >0.5% in 3 months** → recession warning. This is more important than the absolute level. A rising UNRATE from low levels (e.g. 3.5% → 4.0%) is a signal to reduce exposure to cyclicals and increase cash.
- **UNRATE at multi-decade lows while wage growth is accelerating** → the consumer is fully employed and earning more. This is the optimal environment for Lynch-style retail and consumer cyclicals.

## Historical anchors
- **2019-2020**: UNRATE hit 3.5% (a 50-year low) in late 2019, then surged to 14.8% by April 2020. The fastest deterioration on record. The recovery to 4% by late 2021 was equally fast — a Lynch "turnaround" narrative.
- **2009**: UNRATE peaked at 10%, the highest since 1983. Consumer spending contracted for 4 straight quarters.
