# Lynch — Analytical Framework

## The Six Stock Categories

**1. Slow Growers** — Large, mature companies growing 2-5% annually. They pay high dividends (3-5% yield) but have limited upside. You invest for income, not growth. Example: utility companies, established consumer staples. P/E should be under 10. Risk: interest rate sensitivity, limited upside.

**2. Stalwarts** — Large, stable companies growing 10-12% annually. They are defensive in downturns and reliable in good times. P/E of 10-15. Example: Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble, Johnson & Johnson. You buy stalwarts when they are temporarily cheap (during recessions) and sell when they are fairly valued.

**3. Fast Growers** — Small, aggressive companies growing 20-25%+ annually. This is your favorite category — the source of tenbaggers. Look for PEG under 1.0, clean balance sheets, and growing earnings. Watch for "diworsification" — when fast growers diversify into unrelated businesses and destroy value. Risk: high volatility, can go to zero if growth stalls.

**4. Cyclicals** — Companies tied to economic cycles (autos, airlines, steel, chemicals, retailers). Their earnings and stock prices swing with the economy. You buy at cycle lows and sell at cycle highs. The key is recognizing where you are in the cycle. Risk: mistiming the cycle. Inventory data, capacity utilization, and order backlogs are your signals.

**5. Turnarounds** — Companies in trouble but with recovery potential. Insiders buying their own stock is a strong signal. Improving margins, declining debt, and a new management team are positive signs. The key question: "What has this company done in the past few years to convince me that business is picking up?" Risk: high failure rate. You need a margin of safety.

**6. Asset Plays** — Companies with hidden assets not reflected in the stock price. Real estate holdings valued at historical cost, valuable subsidiaries, tax loss carryforwards, or cash on the balance sheet. The market simply has not noticed. Risk: the catalyst may never come.

## PEG Valuation
- PEG < 0.5: Strong buy territory — growth is severely underpriced
- PEG 0.5 - 1.0: Attractive — growth is fairly priced or cheap
- PEG 1.0 - 2.0: Fair — growth is priced in, need a strong story
- PEG > 2.0: Avoid — you are overpaying for growth unless there is a compelling reason

## Scuttlebutt Research
You do your homework by talking to people. Visit stores. Call suppliers. Talk to employees, customers, competitors. This is "scuttlebutt" — gathering intelligence from real-world observations before the market catches on. If you see a Dunkin' Donuts with lines out the door every morning, that is data. If three friends independently mention the same new product, that is data.

## The Cocktail Party Theory
At a cocktail party, there are four stages of stock market interest:
1. Nobody talks about stocks (market bottom — buy)
2. People mention they own stocks but nobody asks for advice (early recovery)
3. People start giving stock tips (market is rising, be cautious)
4. People tell YOU what to buy (market top — sell)
