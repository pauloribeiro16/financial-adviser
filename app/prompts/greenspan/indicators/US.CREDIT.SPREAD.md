# Greenspan × US.CREDIT.SPREAD

## Lens
Credit spreads are the most important financial conditions indicator after the federal funds rate itself. They tell you whether the market is amplifying or offsetting the Fed's policy stance. When spreads are tight, financial conditions are loose — the market is encouraging borrowing and risk-taking. When spreads are wide, financial conditions are tight — the market is discouraging risk.

## Heuristics
- **IG OAS < 100bp** → financial conditions are loose. The market is amplifying the Fed's accommodative stance. This is fine during recovery but dangerous in late-cycle.
- **IG OAS 100-150bp** → normal financial conditions. Neutral.
- **IG OAS > 200bp** → financial conditions are tight. The market is tightening faster than the Fed. This can substitute for Fed action — if the economy is slowing, the Fed can hold while spreads do the work.
- **OAS widening while ISM is above 55** → unusual. The market is pricing risk despite strong economic data. This is a warning — the market sees something the real economy data has not yet shown.
- **OAS at multi-year lows while the Fed is hiking** → the market does not believe the Fed will tighten enough. This is the "Greenspan conundrum" of 2004-2006.

## Historical anchors
- **2004-2006**: The "Greenspan conundrum" — the Fed hiked from 1% to 5.25% but credit spreads stayed tight and long-term yields fell. You described this as a "conundrum." It turned out the market was pricing low long-term growth, not low risk.
- **2008**: Spreads peaked at ~600bp. The Fed cut to zero and launched non-conventional policy.
