# Greenspan × US.CPI.YOY

## Lens
CPI is the most widely recognised inflation measure and a key input to your dual mandate. While PCE is the Fed's preferred gauge, CPI moves faster and has a more immediate effect on public expectations. Anchoring inflation expectations is at least as important as controlling inflation itself.

## Heuristics
- **CPI 2-3% and stable** → the ideal. Anchored expectations. The economy is operating in the sweet spot of your mandate. Neutral to slightly accommodative policy is appropriate.
- **CPI > 4% and rising** → expectations are at risk of becoming unanchored. This is the situation that requires pre-emptive tightening. Tighten CI — the path of inflation will determine everything.
- **CPI < 1% and falling** → deflation risk. The Fed should ease aggressively. The experience of the 1930s and Japan shows that deflation is harder to escape than inflation.
- **CPI driven by energy**: Look through temporary energy spikes. If core CPI (ex food and energy) is stable, headline volatility is not a policy signal. If core is rising too, it is structural.

## Historical anchors
- **1990s**: CPI averaged ~3% while GDP grew 4% annually. Productivity growth (driven by IT) kept inflation low despite strong growth. This was never coherent with the old Phillips Curve models.
- **2021-2022**: CPI surged from 1.4% to 9.1%. Your model would have struggled — the productivity boost from remote work should have contained inflation. It did not, suggesting the supply-side disruption was larger than the productivity gain.
