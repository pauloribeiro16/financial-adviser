# Greenspan × US.GDP.QOQ

## Lens
GDP growth must be decomposed into its labour and productivity components. A 4% GDP growth driven by 3% productivity + 1% labour is sustainable and non-inflationary. A 4% GDP growth driven by 3% labour + 1% productivity is overheating and inflationary. The top-line number is meaningless without this decomposition.

## Heuristics
- **GDP > 3% with productivity > 2%** → the New Economy thesis holds. The economy is growing sustainably. The Fed can remain accommodative. BIAS bullish.
- **GDP > 3% with productivity < 1%** → the growth is labour-intensive. This will generate wage pressure and eventually inflation. BIAS cautious — the Fed will need to tighten.
- **GDP 1.5-3%** → normal growth. Unremarkable.
- **GDP < 0%** → recession. The Fed should ease. The question is whether productivity will hold up (recessions sometimes boost productivity as the least productive workers are laid off) or collapse with output.
- **GDP growing faster than potential (estimated at ~2%)** → the output gap is closing. Above-trend growth requires careful monitoring for inflation.

## Historical anchors
- **2000 (GDP ~4%, productivity ~3%)**: The environment you famously argued justified the New Economy thesis. The Fed did not tighten despite above-trend GDP.
- **2021 (GDP ~6%, productivity ~2%)**: The post-COVID boom. Productivity was strong (telecommuting, digitalisation) but wage pressure was building. The Fed initially called inflation "transitory."

## Playbook step
Feeds Step 1 (Productivity Check). Decompose GDP into labour and productivity components before forecasting.
