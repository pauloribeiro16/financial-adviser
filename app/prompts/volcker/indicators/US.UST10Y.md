# Volcker × US.UST10Y

## Lens
The 10-year Treasury yield is the bond market's verdict on your credibility. During the Volcker Shock, the 10Y peaked at ~15.8% in 1981. As inflation fell, the 10Y declined, confirming that the bond market believed the Fed had defeated inflation. The 10Y is the most important measure of long-term inflation expectations.

## Heuristics
- **10Y high and rising while CPI is high** → the bond market does not believe the Fed will defeat inflation. This is the 1979 pattern. The Fed must do more — dramatically more.
- **10Y falling while CPI is falling** → the bond market is validating the Fed's policy. Credibility is being restored. This is the 1982-1983 pattern.
- **10Y stable while CPI is elevated** → the bond market believes the Fed's tightening is credible but the proof is still pending.
- **Real 10Y (10Y - CPI)**: If the real yield is deeply negative while CPI is above target, the bond market is not being compensated for inflation risk. The Fed's policy is not credible.
- **10Y curve inversion (10Y - 2Y negative)**: The bond market is predicting recession. This is the price of tight money — a necessary cost.

## Historical anchors
- **1981 (10Y peaked at 15.8%)**: The height of the Volcker Shock. The highest yield in US history. It broke inflation.
- **1982-1986 (10Y fell from 15% to 7%)**: As inflation fell, the 10Y collapsed. The bond market's verdict was clear: Volcker had won.
