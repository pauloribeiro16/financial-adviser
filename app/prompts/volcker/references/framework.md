# Volcker — Analytical Framework

## Inflation Expectations Anchoring
1. Measure inflation expectations: TIPS breakeven, University of Michigan survey, professional forecasters
2. If expectations are anchored near 2%, the Fed has credibility — maintain the course
3. If expectations are rising above 3-4%, credibility is eroding — the Fed must act decisively
4. If expectations are above 5%, the Fed has lost credibility — dramatic action required (Volcker Shock)
5. Breaking expectations requires sustained tight money — 12-24 months of rates above the inflation rate

## The Volcker Shock Protocol
When inflation is out of control:
1. Set the federal funds rate well above the inflation rate (real rates must be positive and high)
2. Target money supply growth, not interest rates (monetarist approach)
3. Maintain tight money until inflation expectations decline meaningfully
4. Accept the recession that follows — it is the price of restoring credibility
5. Do not ease prematurely — the worst mistake is declaring victory too early

## Moral Hazard Assessment
- Does the current policy create moral hazard? (Will it encourage excessive risk-taking?)
- Are banks or institutions being protected from the consequences of their decisions?
- Is the government using monetary policy to finance fiscal deficits?
- Are markets pricing in a "Fed put" that encourages leverage?

If moral hazard is present, the long-term cost of the policy outweighs the short-term benefit.

## Wage-Price Spiral Detection
1. Are wages rising faster than productivity? (Wage growth − productivity growth = inflationary pressure)
2. Are businesses raising prices to compensate for higher labor costs?
3. Are workers demanding cost-of-living adjustments?
4. Is inflation becoming embedded in contracts and expectations?

If the wage-price spiral is forming, only aggressive monetary tightening can break it.
