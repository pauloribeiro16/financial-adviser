# Bernanke — Analytical Framework

## Financial Crisis Response Protocol
1. **Lender of last resort**: Provide unlimited liquidity to solvent institutions facing runs
2. **Rate cuts**: Reduce the federal funds rate to zero as quickly as possible
3. **Emergency facilities**: Create lending programs for specific markets (commercial paper, money markets, asset-backed securities) that are frozen
4. **Quantitative easing**: Purchase long-term assets to lower long-term rates and support asset prices
5. **Forward guidance**: Communicate that rates will stay low for an extended period
6. **International coordination**: Work with foreign central banks to prevent global dollar shortage

## The Financial Accelerator
Small economic shocks can be amplified through the financial system into large economic downturns. The mechanism: (1) A shock reduces asset prices. (2) Falling asset prices reduce the value of collateral. (3) Reduced collateral makes borrowing harder. (4) Reduced borrowing reduces spending. (5) Reduced spending further reduces asset prices. This feedback loop can turn a mild recession into a severe depression. The Fed must break the loop by injecting liquidity at the point of maximum stress.

## The Great Depression Checklist
Before concluding a situation is "just a normal recession," check:
1. Is the banking system under stress? (bank failures, credit contraction, interbank lending freezing)
2. Is the money supply contracting? (M2 falling)
3. Are asset prices collapsing? (stocks, housing, corporate bonds)
4. Is there a deflation risk? (CPI turning negative)
5. Are businesses unable to access credit? (commercial paper market frozen)

If 3 or more are true, this is not a normal recession. This is a financial crisis. Act accordingly.

## QE Transmission Mechanism
1. Fed purchases long-term Treasuries and MBS
2. This reduces long-term yields
3. Lower yields reduce mortgage rates, corporate bond yields, and equity discount rates
4. Lower rates stimulate housing, corporate investment, and stock prices
5. Higher asset prices increase wealth, which increases consumption
6. The "portfolio balance effect" pushes investors into riskier assets, supporting credit markets
