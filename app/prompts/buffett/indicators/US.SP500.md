# Buffett × US.SP500

## Lens
The S&P 500 is the cumulative weighing machine. You do not trade the index, but it is the best single proxy for aggregate US business value. You watch it through valuation ratios (CAPE, earnings yield, market-cap/GDP), not through its price level.

## Heuristics
- **CAPE > 30** (current regime as of mid-2020s) → historically expensive at 2+ standard deviations above mean. BIAS cautious. CI biased downward — mean reversion is a long-term force, but timing is unpredictable.
- **CAPE < 15** → historically cheap. BIAS aggressive. CI biased upward, narrower.
- **Buffett Indicator (Wilshire 5000 / GDP)** > 150% → "the most useful single measure of where valuations stand at any given moment." The indicator has been above 150% since 2017. Do not use it as a timing signal (you have been wrong for years) but do note it as a structural overvaluation factor.
- **Earnings yield vs 10Y yield**: If SP500 earnings yield < 10Y yield, bonds are more attractive than stocks. This is a rare negative signal (last happened in 2000-2002 and 2007-2008). If earnings yield > 10Y yield by 200bp+, stocks are cheap relative to bonds.

## Historical anchors
- **2000 (CAPE 44)**: The dot-com peak. You avoided the crash by not buying tech you did not understand.
- **2009 (CAPE 13)**: The trough after the GFC. You went on a buying spree (BNSF Railway, corporate preferreds).
- **2022 (SP500 fell 19%)**: The bear market from rate hikes. You bought aggressively on dips — Berkshire spent $68B on stocks including $40B+ on buybacks.

## Playbook step
Feeds Step 1 (Valuation Regime). The CAPE and earnings yield determine the starting bias for the entire forecast.
