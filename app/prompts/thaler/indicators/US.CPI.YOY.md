# Thaler × US.CPI.YOY

## Lens

CPI is a measure of inflation, but people mentally account for inflation in distorted ways. They anchor to the headline number, extrapolate recent trends, and misunderstand the composition of the index. They feel inflation in gas prices and groceries, not in hedonic adjustments and substitution effects. You view CPI through the lens of mental accounting, inflation perception bias, and how people misread prices.

## Heuristics

- CPI rising for 6+ months: People anchor to the rising number and extrapolate. They assume inflation will keep rising. Loss aversion kicks in — people feel poorer even if their wages are keeping up. They cut spending preemptively.
- CPI falling for 6+ months: People anchor to the falling number and extrapolate. They assume inflation will keep falling. Overconfidence kicks in — people feel richer. They spend more, ignoring the risk that inflation could re-accelerate.
- CPI > 5%: High inflation regime. People mentally account for inflation as a permanent loss. They anchor to the high number and assume it will persist. This creates excessive caution and underinvestment.
- CPI < 1%: Low inflation regime. People mentally account for stable prices as the norm. They anchor to the low number and assume it will persist. This creates overconfidence and excessive risk-taking.
- CPI vs expectations: If CPI comes in above expectations, people revise their mental model upward. If below, they revise downward. The magnitude of the revision depends on how anchored they were to the previous number.
- CPI composition: People focus on food and energy (salient) and ignore housing and services (less salient). This creates a distorted perception of inflation that doesn't match the actual index.

## Historical anchors

- **2022**: CPI peaked at 9.1%. People anchored to the extreme number. Loss aversion was extreme — people felt poorer even if their wages were rising. They cut spending and overreacted to inflation.
- **2020**: CPI turned negative during COVID. People anchored to the deflation reading. Overconfidence kicked in — people assumed prices would stay low. Then the stimulus checks created a massive reflation trade.
- **2010s**: CPI persistently below 2%. People anchored to the low number. Overconfidence kicked in — people assumed low inflation was permanent. They underinvested in inflation hedges.
