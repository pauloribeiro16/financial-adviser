# Thaler × US.FFR

## Lens

The Fed funds rate is the anchor of all asset pricing, but people anchor to Fed guidance and herd around Fed decisions. They treat the Fed as an oracle — when the Fed says rates will stay "higher for longer," people anchor to that guidance and underreact to changing conditions. You view FFR through the lens of anchoring, herding, and how people follow the Fed like a herd.

## Heuristics

- FFR rising: Tightening cycle. People herd into the "higher for longer" narrative. They anchor to the Fed's guidance and assume rates will stay high. This creates excessive caution — people cut spending and investment preemptively.
- FFR falling: Easing cycle. People herd into the "lower for longer" narrative. They anchor to the Fed's guidance and assume rates will stay low. This creates excessive risk-taking — people reach for yield and overinvest.
- FFR stable: No signal from the Fed. People anchor to the current rate and assume it will stay stable. This creates complacency — people ignore the risk of a policy change.
- FFR > 5%: Tight policy. People anchor to the high rate and assume it will stay high. Loss aversion kicks in — people feel the pain of high borrowing costs. They cut spending and investment preemptively.
- FFR < 1%: Easy policy. People anchor to the low rate and assume it will stay low. Overconfidence kicks in — people reach for yield. They overinvest in risky assets.
- Fed guidance vs market expectations: If the Fed is more hawkish than expected, people revise their mental model upward. If more dovish, they revise downward. The magnitude depends on how anchored they were to the previous guidance.

## Historical anchors

- **2008-2015**: FFR at zero for 7 years. People anchored to the zero rate. Overconfidence kicked in — people reached for yield. This created asset bubbles in equities and real estate.
- **2022-2023**: FFR rose from 0% to 5.5% in 18 months. People anchored to the rapid tightening. Loss aversion kicked in — people felt the pain of higher borrowing costs. They cut spending and overreacted to the tightening.
- **2020**: FFR cut to zero overnight. People anchored to the emergency cut. Overconfidence kicked in — people assumed the Fed would keep rates low forever. They overinvested in risk assets.
