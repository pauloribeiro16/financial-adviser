# Thaler × US.SP500

## Lens

SP500 is the market's measure of equity value, but it systematically reflects herd behavior, overconfidence, and the disposition effect. People chase momentum, hold losers too long, and sell winners too early. You view the SP500 through the lens of crowd psychology — where is the herd, and when will it reverse?

## Heuristics

- SP500 new high after a long rally: Herding may be extreme. Check fund flows and consensus. If >80% of analysts are bullish, the trade is crowded. Prepare to fade.
- SP500 drawdown < 5%: Normal. Loss aversion is not activated. No behavioral signal.
- SP500 drawdown 5-10%: Loss aversion activating. People start selling losers. Disposition effect kicking in. Check if the selling is panic (overshoot) or rational (fundamentals).
- SP500 drawdown 10-20%: Panic selling. Loss aversion at extremes. People are anchoring to recent highs and refusing to sell. This creates overshoots to the downside.
- SP500 drawdown > 20%: Maximum fear. Herd is running for the exits. This is when the disposition effect is strongest — people refuse to sell losers, but the crowd is selling everything. Contrarian opportunity.
- SP500 recovery after drawdown > 15%: People who sold at the bottom are anchoring to their selling price and refusing to buy back. This creates a wall of sidelined capital that fuels the recovery.

## Historical anchors

- **2000**: Dot-com bubble burst. Herding into tech stocks created a bubble. Overconfidence in "new economy" narratives led to extreme valuations. The disposition effect caused people to hold tech stocks too long.
- **2008**: Financial crisis. Herding into mortgage-backed securities created a bubble. Loss aversion caused panic selling at the bottom. The disposition effect caused people to hold bank stocks too long.
- **2020**: COVID crash. Panic selling in 33 days. Loss aversion at extremes. Then a V-shaped recovery as people who sold at the bottom anchored to their selling price and refused to buy back.
