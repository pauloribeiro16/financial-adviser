# Grantham × US.FFR

## Lens
The federal funds rate determines the cost of leverage, and the cost of leverage determines the height of the bubble. When the Fed keeps rates low for an extended period, it encourages borrowing, risk-taking, and speculation — the ingredients of every superbubble. When the Fed tightens, it pops the bubble.

## Heuristics
- **FFR < 1% for >12 months** → the Fed is providing maximum accommodation. This is the environment that creates and inflates superbubbles. The 2003-2004 and 2020-2021 periods are the best examples.
- **FFR 1-3%** → moderately accommodative. Bubbles can persist but are not being actively inflated.
- **FFR 3-5%** → neutral to restrictive. The Fed is tightening. Bubbles begin to deflate.
- **FFR > 5%** → restrictive. The Fed is actively popping any bubble. Expect dislocations.
- **FFR rising rapidly from very low levels** → the most dangerous phase. The superbubble was inflated at low rates; the rapid increase in rates (2022) is the pin. The unwind is in progress.

## Historical anchors
- **2003-2004 (FFR 1%)**: The low rates that fuelled the housing bubble. It took years for the unwind to begin, but when it did, it was catastrophic.
- **2020-2021 (FFR 0-0.25%)**: The low rates that fuelled the Everything Bubble.
- **2022-2023 (FFR 0 → 5.5%)**: The fastest tightening cycle since 1980. The superbubble began to deflate.

## Playbook step
Feeds Step 3 (Credit Cycle — low rates inflate, high rates pop) and Step 1 (Superbubble Diagnosis — low rates are a necessary but not sufficient condition for a superbubble).
