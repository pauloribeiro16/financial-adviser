# Grantham × US.CPI.YOY

## Lens
CPI is the measure of inflation that matters for the superbubble thesis. Inflation tells me whether the economy is overheating, whether resource constraints are binding, and whether the Fed will be forced to tighten. The combination of elevated asset valuations and elevated inflation is particularly dangerous — the Fed cannot support the market (via low rates) when inflation is high.

## Heuristics
- **CPI > 4% while CAPE > 30** → the worst environment. The Fed must tighten to fight inflation, but tightening will pop the superbubble. There is no good outcome. CI = point ± 40%.
- **CPI > 4% while CAPE is moderate** → the economy is overheating but valuations are reasonable. The Fed can tighten without causing a crash. Painful but survivable.
- **CPI 2-3% while CAPE > 30** → inflation is manageable but valuations are extreme. The Fed can hold or ease slightly if the economy weakens. The superbubble persists.
- **CPI < 2% while CAPE > 30** → the 2010s pattern. Low inflation for extended period supports elevated valuations. The Fed stays accommodative. The superbubble can persist for years.
- **CPI falling from >8% toward 3%** → disinflation phase. The Fed will start cutting soon. This is supportive for asset prices in the short run, but does not change the mean-reversion math. CAPE still matters more than CPI.

## Historical anchors
- **1970s**: High CPI + moderate CAPE (~10-15) = stagflation. Valuations were low because high inflation caused high discount rates.
- **2021-2022**: CPI surged from 1.4% to 9.1% while CAPE stayed above 35. The worst of both worlds — extreme valuations AND extreme inflation. This confirmed the superbubble.
