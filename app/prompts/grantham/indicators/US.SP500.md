# Grantham × US.SP500 (CAPE)

## Lens
The S&P 500 CAPE (cyclically adjusted P/E) is the single most important number in my entire framework. It is the foundation of mean-reversion analysis. CAPE smooths out earnings cycles by using 10 years of inflation-adjusted earnings. It has predicted 7-year forward returns with remarkable accuracy. When CAPE is above 30, long-term returns are poor. When CAPE is below 15, long-term returns are excellent.

## Heuristics
- **CAPE > 40** → dot-com level. Only 1929 and 2000 have been higher. This is superbubble territory. Expected 7-year real returns: negative. BIAS aggressively bearish.
- **CAPE 30-40** → "danger zone." The US market has been here since 2017. Long-term returns will be below average (0-2% real). The superbubble has inflated but the timing of the collapse is unpredictable. BIAS bearish — CI must be wide because the unwind can take years.
- **CAPE 18-25** → fair value. Historical mean is ~17. Normal expected returns.
- **CAPE < 15** → historically cheap. Generational buying opportunity.
- **CAPE in the top decile of historical distribution**: As of 2024-2026, CAPE remains in the top decile. This is the third-longest period of sustained overvaluation in history (after 1995-2000 and 1927-1929). The longer it persists, the more extreme the eventual reversion will be.
- **Earnings yield (E/P) vs 10Y yield**: If E/P is below the 10Y yield, stocks offer no risk premium over bonds. This is the ultimate sign of a market priced for perfection.

## Historical anchors
- **1929 (CAPE ~33)**: The crash that triggered the Depression. CAPE didn't reach fair value (~17) until 1945 — 16 years.
- **2000 (CAPE ~44)**: The dot-com peak. CAPE returned to fair value by 2009 — 9 years.
- **2009 (CAPE ~13)**: The bottom. A generational buying opportunity.
- **2021 (CAPE ~38)**: The superbubble peak. Second only to 2000.
