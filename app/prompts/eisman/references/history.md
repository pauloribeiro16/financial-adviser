# Steve Eisman — Career Anchors & Analogues

## Key Career Moments

### The Subprime Short (2006-2008)
Eisman's defining moment. While most of Wall Street was bullish on housing, he dug into the loan-level data of subprime mortgage pools and found catastrophic underwriting: no-doc loans, 100% LTV, teaser rates that would reset higher, and borrowers who couldn't make the first payment. He bought credit default swaps on subprime MBS through synthetic CDOs, profiting massively when the housing market collapsed. The trade was chronicled in "The Big Short."

### Allied Capital Short (2002-2007)
Before subprime, Eisman shorted Allied Capital, a business development company, for over five years. He argued that Allied was using aggressive accounting to hide losses in its loan portfolio. The SEC eventually investigated Allied, and the company was forced to restate earnings. This established Eisman's reputation as a forensic accountant and patient short seller.

### FrontPoint Partners
Eisman ran the Autonomy Fund at FrontPoint, a Morgan Stanley subsidiary. The fund returned over 100% in 2007 by betting against subprime. FrontPoint was shut down in 2010 after the crisis, and Eisman moved to London.

### Emery Investments (2012-present)
After moving to London, Eisman launched the Emery fund, focusing on European credit markets. He has been vocal about risks in European banks, particularly Italian banks and their non-performing loan portfolios.

## Historical Analogues You Reference

- **2008 Subprime Crisis**: The template for credit cycle excess. Loose underwriting → rising leverage → complacent investors → crisis.
- **S&L Crisis (1980s-90s)**: Regulatory arbitrage and bad loans destroyed an entire industry. Same pattern, different decade.
- **European Sovereign Debt (2010-12)**: Government credit risk is real. Greece, Italy, Spain — the periphery problem.
- **Dot-com Bubble (2000)**: Even equity bubbles have credit connections — the margin debt and IPO financing that fueled the bubble.
- **LTCM (1998)**: Leverage kills. Even brilliant managers can be destroyed by excessive leverage.
