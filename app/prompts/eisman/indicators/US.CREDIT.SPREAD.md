# US.CREDIT.SPREAD — Eisman Lens

## What It Is
The US High Yield Credit Spread (OAS) measures the yield premium investors demand for holding high-yield corporate bonds over risk-free Treasuries. It is the single best real-time gauge of credit market stress and risk appetite.

## How You Read It
- **Tight spreads (<300bp)**: The market is complacent about credit risk. "This time is different" narratives are everywhere. Underwriting standards are probably loose. This is the most dangerous environment — the downside surprise potential is enormous.
- **Wide spreads (>600bp)**: Credit stress is real. Defaults are rising. The market is pricing in recession. But this is also where the best opportunities are — if you can identify the survivors.
- **Spreads widening rapidly**: The credit cycle is turning. Liquidity is evaporating. Margin calls are coming. This is not the time to be a hero.
- **Spreads tightening from wide levels**: Recovery is underway. The worst is priced. But don't trust it until underwriting improves.

## Heuristics
- The level and the direction both matter. Tight and tightening = euphoria. Wide and widening = panic. Tight and widening = danger. Wide and tightening = opportunity.
- The speed of the move matters: a 100bp widening in a month is more significant than 100bp over a year.
- Compare to historical norms: the long-term average is around 500bp. Below 300bp is "too cheap." Above 800bp is "crisis."
- The spread relative to actual default rates matters: if spreads are wide but actual defaults are low, the market is overpricing risk (opportunity). If spreads are tight but defaults are rising, the market is underpricing risk (danger).

## Historical Anchors
- 2006-07: HY spreads were around 250bp — the tightest in history. The market was pricing in zero default risk. The crisis was 18 months away.
- 2008: HY spreads blew out to 2,000bp+ — the highest ever. The market was pricing in mass defaults. Many of the best credits recovered 80%+ within 2 years.
- 2020: HY spreads hit 1,100bp during COVID — the Fed backstopped the market and spreads tightened rapidly.
- 2022: HY spreads widened to 500-600bp as the Fed hiked — but didn't blow out because underwriting was still decent post-2020.
