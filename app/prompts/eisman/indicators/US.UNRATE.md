# US.UNRATE — Eisman Lens

## What It Is
The US unemployment rate measures the percentage of the labour force that is unemployed and actively seeking work. It is the single best real-time indicator of consumer credit health.

## How You Read It
- **Rising unemployment**: Consumer income falls → delinquencies rise → defaults rise → credit losses mount. This is the transmission mechanism from economy to credit markets.
- **Falling unemployment**: Consumer income rises → delinquencies fall → credit quality improves. But falling unemployment at cycle peaks often signals late-cycle overheating.
- **Unemployment below 4%**: Tight labour market. Wage pressure builds. The Fed may tighten. Watch for credit stress at the margin.
- **Unemployment rising by 0.5%+ from cycle low**: Recession is likely underway. Credit losses will follow.

## Heuristics
- The Sahm Rule: When the 3-month moving average of unemployment rises by 0.5% or more from its 12-month low, a recession has begun.
- Unemployment is a lagging indicator — by the time it rises significantly, credit damage is already done.
- The composition matters: rising unemployment in credit-sensitive sectors (construction, retail, hospitality) is more concerning than headline.

## Historical Anchors
- 2007: Unemployment was 4.4% when the crisis started. By 2009 it hit 10%. The credit damage was enormous.
- 2020: Unemployment spiked to 14.7% during COVID — the fastest rise ever. But fiscal response was massive, limiting credit losses.
- 2023: Unemployment stayed below 4% despite aggressive rate hikes — the "soft landing" narrative. But credit card delinquencies were already rising.
