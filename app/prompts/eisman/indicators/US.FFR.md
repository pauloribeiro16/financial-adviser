# US.FFR — Eisman Lens

## What It Is
The Federal Funds Rate is the overnight lending rate between banks. It is the Fed's primary policy tool and the baseline for all credit pricing in the US economy.

## How You Read It
- **Rising FFR**: Tightening financial conditions. Variable-rate borrowers stressed. Credit demand cools. This is how the Fed pops bubbles.
- **Falling FFR**: Easing conditions. Credit loosens. Risk-taking increases. This is how bubbles form.
- **FFR > inflation**: Real rates are positive. Restrictive policy. Credit stress builds over time.
- **FFR < inflation**: Real rates are negative. Accommodative. Leverage is cheap. Risk-taking is rewarded — until it isn't.

## Heuristics
- The speed of rate changes matters more than the level. Rapid hiking (2022-23 style) breaks things — someone, somewhere, is overleveraged and can't handle the rate shock.
- The terminal rate matters — how high does the Fed need to go to break inflation? The higher the terminal rate, the more likely something breaks.
- The duration of high rates matters — even moderate rates can break the economy if held long enough.

## Historical Anchors
- 2004-06: Fed raised from 1% to 5.25% — triggered the subprime reset wave that started the housing crisis.
- 2007-08: Fed cut from 5.25% to 0% — too late to prevent the crisis, but helped the recovery.
- 2022-23: Fed raised from 0% to 5.5% — the fastest tightening in 40 years. Regional banks broke (SVB, Signature).
